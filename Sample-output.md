# Research Synthesis Report

**Research topic:** Users navigating subscription downgrade flows
**Date:** claude
**Interviews:** 3
**Confidence:** Medium

---

## Primary Theme

> Downgrade options are systematically hidden behind cancellation flows, causing users who want to stay to nearly leave entirely

## Executive Summary

All three participants successfully downgraded their subscriptions but nearly canceled entirely due to poor UX that buried downgrade options while prominently featuring cancellation. Participants were not churning users—they wanted to maintain their subscriptions at appropriate price points—but the flow treated downgrades as failures rather than retention wins. Post-downgrade satisfaction was universally high, with users reporting they should have downgraded sooner and expressing long-term loyalty at the lower tier.

## Themes

### 1. Downgrade paths buried while cancellation is prominent

**Evidence:** 3 interviews

All participants struggled to find downgrade options, with the path hidden under multiple menu layers or only surfaced during cancellation flows. This design pattern nearly caused complete churn from users who wanted to remain customers.

**Verbatim quotes:**

> *"The cancel button is super prominent, but the downgrade option was buried under like, 'Manage subscription,' then 'Change plan.' I almost gave up and just canceled, honestly."*

> *"I found the cancel option first, because that was clearly labeled. But I didn't want to cancel. I wanted to keep watching, I just wanted a different plan."*

> *"To find the downgrade I had to go through the cancellation flow far enough that they were offering it as a last-ditch attempt. That's bad design."*

### 2. Excessive confirmation requests feel disrespectful

**Evidence:** 3 interviews

Multiple confirmation screens asking users if they're sure about downgrading created frustration and made users feel the company didn't trust their decision-making, particularly when warnings highlighted features they weren't using.

**Verbatim quotes:**

> *"They asked me — three times, I think? — if I was sure. And each time it was like, 'Are you sure? You'll lose 4K!' 'Are you sure? You'll only be able to watch on two devices at once!' And I'm like... yes, I'm sure, I told you twice already, those are the things I don't need."*

> *"They kept asking me if I was sure. And they showed me things I'd lose, but a lot of those things I didn't even know I had."*

> *"I click 'Cancel subscription.' It asks me why. Fine, I pick a reason. It then shows me a screen that's like, 'Did you know about all these features you're losing?' with like seven bullet points. I click continue."*

### 3. Retention offers appreciated but misaligned with user needs

**Evidence:** 3 interviews

Participants valued being recognized as long-time customers through retention offers, but discounts on premium tiers didn't solve the core problem of paying for unused features. Users wanted right-sized plans, not discounted wrong-sized plans.

**Verbatim quotes:**

> *"The offer wasn't going to fix my underlying problem, which is that I don't watch enough to justify Premium even at a discount."*

> *"I'd still be paying for things I don't use, just at a discount. And in three months it would go back to full price and I'd be in the same situation."*

> *"None of them addressed why I was leaving. I wasn't leaving because Premium was too expensive. I was leaving because I no longer needed Premium. Those are different problems."*

### 4. Post-downgrade satisfaction is high with no regret

**Evidence:** 3 interviews

All participants reported being happy after downgrading, noticing minimal quality differences, and expressing long-term loyalty at the lower tier. Downgrades were not slow cancellations but successful right-sizing.

**Verbatim quotes:**

> *"I am not on my way out. I will probably be on the Standard tier for the next five years honestly. The math finally caught up to me but the value's still there at the right price."*

> *"I'm happy. I'm paying about half of what I was paying. The picture quality is fine on my old TV — I genuinely cannot tell the difference."*

> *"Better, actually. Which is interesting. I think because the relationship feels more right-sized. I'm not paying for things I don't use. I'm not feeling resentful every time the charge hits my card."*

### 5. Missed opportunity for proactive tier recommendations

**Evidence:** 2 interviews

Users were on wrong tiers for extended periods (years in some cases) while the company had usage data showing the mismatch. Participants wanted proactive guidance rather than passive overcharging.

**Verbatim quotes:**

> *"Maybe once a year, they could send me an email that says, 'Hi Diane, based on your usage, you might be on the wrong tier. Here are tiers that might fit better.' That would have been so helpful."*

> *"They knew I wasn't using four streams. They knew I wasn't watching in 4K. They had all that information. And they just kept charging me Premium prices anyway."*

### 6. Downgrade framed as breakup rather than relationship adjustment

**Evidence:** 2 interviews

The tone and design of downgrade flows treated users as if they were leaving rather than staying, creating unnecessary friction and negative sentiment during what should be a positive retention moment.

**Verbatim quotes:**

> *"The whole tone of the flow was like I was breaking up with them, and I wasn't. I was just adjusting the terms."*

> *"Don't make me feel like I'm doing something wrong. I'm choosing to stay with you."*

## Contradictions Across Interviews

- Participants had mixed reactions to retention offers: Priya and Diane appreciated being recognized as long-time customers and felt 'seen' by the offers, while Marcus found the multiple sequential offers during cancellation to be aggressive and annoying. The difference may relate to where in the flow offers appeared (downgrade path vs. cancellation path).
- Technical proficiency affected discovery methods but not ultimate frustration: Diane (less technical) had to Google instructions while Marcus and Priya navigated menus, yet all three expressed similar levels of frustration with the buried options, suggesting the UX problem transcends user sophistication.

## Open Questions for Follow-Up

- What percentage of users who intend to downgrade actually cancel entirely because they cannot find the downgrade option or abandon the process out of frustration?
- How do users who were proactively recommended to downgrade by the company (if any such program exists) compare in satisfaction and retention to those who self-initiated downgrades?
- What is the long-term retention rate of downgraded users compared to users who remained on premium tiers, and does the downgrade experience quality affect that retention?
- Do users who downgrade become more or less likely to recommend the service to others, and does the friction in the downgrade process impact net promoter scores?

## Methodology Notes

Small qualitative sample of 3 participants (ages 28-56, mixed technical proficiency) who downgraded within past 60 days. Findings show strong pattern consistency but limited demographic diversity. No participants who abandoned downgrade attempts were interviewed, creating potential survivorship bias.
